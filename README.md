## Overview

The Beast of Gévaudan (La Bête du Gévaudan) is the historical name given to a man-eating animal — or more likely a series of large canids — that terrorized the former French province of Gévaudan, in the Margeride Mountains of south-central France, between 1764 and 1767. Contemporary accounts describe a tawny, wolf-like creature larger than a wolf, with a long tufted tail and dark dorsal stripe, that targeted the head and neck of solitary herders, mostly women and children. Estimates vary; a 1987 quantitative study counted 210 attacks resulting in 113 deaths and 49 injuries, with 98 of the victims partly eaten. The case escalated into a national crisis under King Louis XV, who dispatched first the Norman wolf-hunter Jean-Charles-Marc-Antoine Vaumesle d'Enneval and then his own gun-bearer François Antoine. Antoine killed a large wolf at the Chazes Abbey on 21 September 1765, but attacks resumed in December. The killings finally ended after local hunter Jean Chastel shot a creature on 19 June 1767 during a hunt organized by the Marquis d'Apcher. Modern scholars overwhelmingly favour wolves (single, multiple, or wolf-dog hybrids) as the explanation.

### François Antoine kills the 'wolf of Chazes' ⭐
> **Date**: 1765-09-21  
> **Location**: Forest near the Chazes Abbey  
> **Witnesses**: 2 (François Antoine (king's gunbearer), François Antoine's nephew)  
On 21 September 1765, François Antoine and his nephew shot a large wolf in the forest of Chazes; the animal was about 1.7 m (5 ft 7 in) long. The carcass was stuffed and sent to the royal court at Versailles. Antoine was rewarded with money and honors. Attacks briefly stopped, but resumed in December.
**Credibility**: high — The killed wolf was not in fact the (or not the only) Beast.
**Sources**: Courrier d'Avignon (Oct 1765); Smith, J. M. (2011), Monsters of the Gévaudan

During a hunt organized by the Marquis d'Apcher on Mont Mouchet, local hunter Jean Chastel shot and killed a large canid. ## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Werewolf / loup-garou** _(Local 18th-century rural folklore)_

Contemporary rural belief that the Beast was a werewolf or supernatural creature, sometimes blamed on a particular suspect (folklore around Jean Chastel's son Antoine).

### Scientific

**Multiple wolves** _(Most modern historians and zoologists, including Jay M. Smith (2011))_

The attacks across three years and 90 × 80 km were almost certainly the work of multiple wolves rather than a single individual, possibly several distinct man-eaters in succession.

**Wolf-dog hybrid** _(Karl-Hans Taake (zoologist, 2010s))_

Some descriptions (size, coat, dorsal stripe) are consistent with a wolf-dog hybrid, possibly resulting from local mastiffs interbreeding with wolves.

**Exotic predator hypothesis** _(Various 19th- and 20th-century popular writers)_

Older speculation suggested an escaped exotic animal (hyena, lion, or trained 'killer dog'). Most historians reject this for lack of evidence.

### Skeptical

**Media-amplified panic over real but ordinary wolf attacks** _(Jay M. Smith (2011), Monsters of the Gévaudan)_

Real wolf attacks were amplified by France's first national news media into a singular 'Beast'; folklore consolidated separate incidents into one creature.
